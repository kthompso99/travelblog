We spent ten days in the Greek islands in early May 2025. Here's what we'd tell ourselves if we were planning it again.

### Going in early May

Early May avoids crowds but arrives before the beach season really starts.

Good news: the crowds were very light, we could just walk up to very popular restaurants right at 7:30pm, especially in Milos and Paros (Santorini was already fairly busy).    

If you want the “Greek Island beach experience”, we found that almost all the beaches were nearly-empty May 3-13.  The beach clubs that define the summer season on the Greek islands open … beginning of June.  In May one sees stacks of lounge chairs that aren’t available yet.  So we weren’t shoulder season, we were off-season.  

It was cool a lot of days (high 60s), sometimes almost chilly when the wind kicked up.  Definitely we were wearing long sleeves in the evening, sometimes in the afternoon.  

We had a lot of wind, but depending on who I ask that might happen all summer – it’s super unpredictable and can change quickly.   One day we had 30–40 mph winds all afternoon, then an hour later it was calm for dinner.

We swam in the Mediterranean exactly once (Santorini catamaran cruise) – it was bracing to say the least.  Doable but hardly “relaxing” water temp.

### Big Picture

The advice to avoid 'too many islands' proved correct. Ferries and hotel transfers carry significant overhead: the 30-minute early arrival, the shuttle logistics, and the repacking.  Anything more than three overnight stops in ten days (2 nights in Milos, 3 nights in Santorini, 5 nights in Paros) might feel like work rather than travel.

But we enjoyed fitting in Naxos for six hours, Delos/Mykonos for three hours each, and Antiparos for a short visit. We only repacked luggage three times but still saw several islands, and we were surprised how different Naxos and Paros felt.  

The contrast is amazing – Milos is quite sleepy, with limited tourist infrastructure, and Pollonia’s “restaurant row” for tourists is 100 yards long.  Santorini is nuts, crawling with tourists, but visually unlike anywhere else.  Paros found a solid middle ground – first-class hotels/restaurants, a lot happening, but feels like a semi-real place that actual Greek people can still live on (Santorini is sad in its way, Oia is almost all tourists now.  One local told me she lives in Imerovigli and hadn’t been to Oia 20 minutes away for years because it’s too expensive and just for tourists).   Santorini is one of the most remarkable sights we’ve ever seen, but Paros was better for simply slowing down and hanging out.

A lot of our “feelings” are probably based on when we went – for example we have friends who visited Milos September 2024 when the beach clubs were open, and Milos was their favorite, in part because of its beaches.  

> In early May, the beach isn’t really “ready” yet (and the water is seriously cold), so for us 48 hours in Milos … was plenty, because there’s only so much to see.

### Ferries

We did only 3 ferries so we are hardly experts – we flew Athens to Milos at the beginning, and Paros to Athens at the end.

We used Ferryhopper to compare routes, but the fastest option for us was always Seajets, so we booked advance tickets directly through their website.   We bought a week in advance, paid the premium for 'club' \-- the Seajets "business class".   It’s less crowded and usually only 10–20% more expensive. It felt worth it.  They had a platinum class for our long ferry (Santorini to Naxos, 2.5 hours) and that had at-seat drink service which wasn't all bad.

You need to check into Seajets online between 48 and 2 hours in advance to get a QR code to scan, just don't forget that or you have to go to an in-person desk (usually near but not right at the ferry dock) to get your code.

### Food & Wine

We’re not experts but a few thoughts.

When you’re in the islands, the “view” restaurants by the water (Milos, Paros) or on the rim (Santorini) tend to be very fish-focused, sometimes with lamb and beef.  

There are often restaurants with ‘fast’ food – gyros and souvlaki and kebabs in various combinations – just a block or two from the seaside views, so decide what you’re looking for and plan accordingly.  Some of this more ‘fast’ food is surprisingly good, we should have eaten more of it.  

Try Moussaka.  Often.  Yemeni in Paros makes a version with a noticeably light, silken bechamel.  

White wine: We like the Monemvasia (grape, not the town), more than Assyrtiko – the latter is tasty, but known for high acid which tired our palate over time.   Malagousia is very tasty too.  

Generally speaking the red wines from the islands … aren’t as amazing in our opinion.

> The islands are simply too warm to grow most red grapes.   We drank a lot more xinomavro from northern Greece when it was cool enough for red.    

We skipped the Ouzo entirely, preferring Mastiha as a refreshing, palate-cleansing after-dinner drink.

Greek desserts are dangerous. Many restaurants have a dessert menu, and when you decline, they bring a free treat anyway that we failed consistently to resist.

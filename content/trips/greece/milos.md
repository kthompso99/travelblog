Milos is quieter than its more famous Cycladic siblings, and wears it well. The tourist infrastructure is modest. That smaller scale gives the island a pace that Santorini and Mykonos traded away long ago. If you're willing to rent a car and explore, Milos easily rewards two or three days.

Most visitors base themselves in one of two towns: Pollonia or Adamantas (Adamas).  Adamantas is the much busier ferry port, while 15-20 minutes away is Pollonia, a quiet fishing village where we stayed.  It has a restaurant row only 100 yards long. 

Milos isn't a "stroll town, stumble into sights" island. It's a "pick a target, drive, and commit" island.

## Where We Stayed

Tania Milos is owner-run in the best sense -- Tania herself is the concierge, the local expert, and the reason we would return. The hotel is positioned far enough from the port to remain quiet, yet the walk to dinner is only eight minutes on level ground. 

![Pollonia, Milos, from the walk towards Tania Milos](images/Milos-02.jpg)

By opting for a sea-view room, you get a clear western vantage point that carries over to the breakfast area, where the tables look directly over the water.  The "Deluxe Room with Full Sunset Sea View" is the most expensive room in the hotel.  But at under $300 a night in shoulder season, the balcony sunset views made it feel like a bargain.

![Sunset from our balcony at Tania Milos](images/Milos-06.jpg)

## Getting Around

Rent a car -- Milos doesn't really work without one.  There were few taxis, and the island's sites and beaches are spread out.

Driving is straightforward on the main roads; the stress comes from the last mile to beaches. Don't accept the "upgrade" to a bigger car -- many roads are narrow, and the extra width becomes stress.  Also the rental agency told us you couldn't take the car off-road.

> We assume everyone must ignore the rental requirements, because so many of the popular beaches to visit are, in fact, off-road.

Be careful driving near Plaka.  On GPS, avoid "fastest" -- it will happily route you onto narrow lanes that are barely car-width.

## Exploring Milos

The signature day trip on Milos is a catamaran cruise, as many of the iconic sites are undrivable.  We booked ours with Odysseus Cruises.  They cancelled at 7pm the night before due to lack of staffing. So we can't report on it firsthand.  

If you're interested in art history, it's worth driving to the capital, Plaka, to see a replica of the Venus de Milo -- known locally as Aphrodite de Milos -- overlooking the water near where she was discovered.  She has a great view of the water from her hillside perch.

## Beaches of Milos
  
The beaches are spread out and varied. I made a quick map [here](https://www.google.com/maps/d/edit?mid=10STMCKq4Tnfg4n-Z_B0SCEIKhQogt8I&usp=sharing) based on some online recommendations of "best beach in Milos".
  
Sarakiniko beach deserves special attention. It's a 5-8 minute walk downhill from the parking lot and one of the most unusual beaches we saw in the Cyclades.  The island winds have carved its smooth, grayish-white volcanic rocks into shapes unlike anything else, the moonscape comparison is accurate.  

![Sarakiniko Beach](images/Milos-03.jpg)

The rock formations have created a natural amphitheater, but calling it a beach feels misleading -- think in terms of clambering over rounded rock terraces, not setting your umbrella.

The day we were there, a small food truck served snacks and drinks by the parking lot.

![The swimming hole of Sarakiniko beach](images/Milo11.jpg)

Milos is quite rugged and hilly. For example, Fyriplaka beach looks close to Paliochori on the map, but you can't drive along the coast -- you drive back into the interior and then out again.  The road down from the interior to Fyriplaka is hairy, you're going down a steep road right near a quarry with massive trucks around you.

## Food and Drink

Among the short row of options in Pollonia, Yialos is the most frequent recommendation from residents. It is a more polished version of the typical Greek fish tavern, with a better-than-expected wine list. Some sites (e.g. [TripAdvisor](https://www.tripadvisor.com/Restaurant_Review-g1105860-d1797014-Reviews-Gialos_restaurant-Pollonia_Milos_Cyclades_South_Aegean.html)) list this as "Gialos", but the menu spelled it with a Y. 

There is an Asian (sushi) restaurant in Pollonia called Hanabi, the locals were unenthusiastic about it, but we enjoyed it as a change from the Greek fish restaurants which all have similar menus.

Medusa restaurant in Mandraki is famous enough to attract celebrities.  It justifies the 15-minute detour from Pollonia for lunch (and likely dinner), with a terrace right above the waterline coupled with fresh, traditional food.

[Kostankis](https://kostantakiswinery.com/) winery is a 10-min walk from Pollonia and the only winery on Milos. Its winery overlooks a small rustic vineyard of local varieties. 

![The vineyards of Kostankis](images/Milos-07.jpg)

Tastings are relaxed and informal, held in a family-run garden.

![Our friendly tasting advisor](images/Milos-08.jpg)

Milos delivered on its promise of a quieter pace. The short restaurant row in Pollonia, the family-run winery, the lack of tour-bus infrastructure -- these aren't gaps in development. They're the island's appeal. If you're deciding between Milos and a more famous Cycladic stop, the question isn't whether Milos is "good enough". It's whether you want that extra layer of calm.
